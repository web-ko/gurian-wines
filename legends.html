BAISSE VOILE

BAISSE VOILE

The legend at the heart of Lanovelle's identity

Imagine the château at the beginning of the 17th century… The first Duke of Épernon has just become its owner. The reputation of this great French admiral was such that as boats passed in front of his estate, they would lower their sails to show their allegiance. This deep mark of respect gave rise to the château’s emblem, a ship with a griffon-shaped prow; its name in Gascon, Bêcha vêla, meaning “baisse voile” (“lower the sails”), later became Lanovelle.

THE OLD CEDAR TREE

THE OLD CEDAR TREE

LANOVELLE, A TRIBUTE TO NATURE

Near the entrance to the château, in the middle of the main courtyard, a huge cedar tree flourishes. It has become increasingly venerable and majestic in its old age. Legend has it that this giant conifer has been standing proudly at Lanovelle for more than 200 years, a symbol of wisdom that, year after year, watches over the property’s precious vines, and the beautiful gardens which surround it, where visitors like to stroll.

A MUSICAL ACCOMPANIMENT

A MUSICAL ACCOMPANIMENT

HARVESTS TO THE SOUND OF THE VIOLON

During the period when the Heine family lived at the château, music resounded through the vineyard with the introduction of a lively tradition… As the sun moved down to the horizon, the full baskets of grapes would be tipped into the open vats. The pickers would sing as they made their way to the press. As the men rolled up their shirt sleeves and trousers, the air would suddenly start to vibrate with the first notes of the violin. The signal to commence given, the men would launch into a merry dance, crushing the grapes with their feet to the clapping of the women and the gentlemen, taking their rhythm and fresh energy from the music. And so it was in those days of strenuous labour, that a good-humoured atmosphere was maintained, under the benevolent eye of the Heine family.

LOUISIANA

LOUISIANA

WHEN LOUISIANA CAME TO THE MÉDOC

In 1860, Armand Heine, the owner of Lanovelle, visited Louisiana where he married Marie-Amélie Kohn. His young American wife suffered so badly from seasickness during their honeymoon trip on the Mississippi that she vowed never to set foot on a boat again once she had arrived in France. Her husband, hoping that the Gironde flowing beside Lanovelle would remind her of her native land, had a new wing built on the left-hand side of the château. Marie-Amélie then decorated it in a style evoking her beloved Louisiana, to ensure that she did not become homesick.

AN EARLY HARVEST

AN EARLY HARVEST

WHEN BOLDNESS SAVED THE CROP

The year was 1964. At that time, Lanovelle had a reputation for being one of the first châteaux in the vicinity to pick its grapes, and certain spreaders of malicious gossip would say that, on account of this, the wines lacked body. But Lanovelle remains true to its convictions and its boldness paid off in 1964 when, on the last day of the château’s early harvest, the skies opened to release torrential rain. The result was catastrophic for the numerous grapes in the surrounding area that were still on the vines. That year, Lanovelle’s wine proved to be one of the finest produced by the great châteaux of the Médoc.

THE SYRINGE METHOD

THE SYRINGE METHOD

WHEN PAINSTAKING CARE OF THE VINES WAS REQUIRED

At the end of the First World War, tending to the vines was no easy task, but Lanovelle was prepared to go to any lengths for its vineyard. At that time, vines were treated with carbon sulphide that was applied with a large syringe. The men formed a line and administered the treatment while the women followed to ensure that it went properly into the soil. Slowly, they would move forward together, taking the greatest possible care of Lanovelle’s vines.
